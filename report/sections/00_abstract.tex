We study whether augmenting the Adaptive Biasing Force (ABF) method with a
Fisher--Rao (FR) birth--death correction improves the computation of a
reaction-coordinate free energy, and, if so, how the correction must be
constructed. On a controlled two-dimensional model potential with reaction
coordinate $\xib(x,y)=x$ and a quadrature-exact reference free energy
$\Fref(x)$, ABF estimates the mean force $F'(x)=\E[\partial_x V\mid X=x]$ while
the FR step gently reallocates particles along $x$ by killing replicas in
over-represented regions and cloning replicas in under-represented regions, so
that the empirical reaction-coordinate marginal $\phat_t(x)$ is nudged toward a
target marginal $q_t(x)$. We compare four methods --- ABF only, and ABF plus FR
with an \emph{estimated}, \emph{uniform}, or \emph{oracle} target --- across a
production study of \NRuns{} GPU runs (\NConfigs{} configurations,
\NSeeds{} seeds, \NParticles{} particles, \NSteps{} steps). The practical,
estimated-target FR yields a \emph{modest} improvement that is clearest in the
time-integrated error $\int_0^T\Norm{\Fhat_t-\Fref}_{\Ltwo}\dd t$
(a reduction of $\EstIntegImpPct\%$ over ABF only at the best configuration),
with smaller final-budget gains; FR is therefore better described as a
convergence accelerator and stabiliser than as a rescue mechanism in this
regime. The oracle target --- which is allowed to use $\Fref$, the very quantity
being computed --- is reported \emph{only as a diagnostic positive control}: it
is not a usable algorithm, but the gap between estimated and oracle targets
($\EstIntegImpPct\%$ versus $\OracIntegImpPct\%$ integrated-error reduction)
indicates that practical performance is bounded by the quality and stability of
the online free-energy target estimate. Across all successful settings the FR
correction is weak (per-event particle-replacement fractions of order
$10^{-5}$), target-consistent, and does not visibly corrupt the conditional
distribution $Y\mid X=x$ on which ABF depends.
